# For Parents: Baby's First Computer

The original version of this program was written in 1994 as a godfather's gift to his friend's daughter: an introduction to the idea that computers respond to humans, even those who are to young to walk and talk. It has been lovingly remade and open-sourced three decades later, for a new generation of babies and the parents who want to sit with them and teach them that they have agency; that they can use a computer to make something change in the world around them.

---

## What the Program Does

Not much, to be honest, but exactly the right amount of stimuli that is needed to teach a baby about interactivity. Baby's First Computer opens a fullscreen window and cycles through eight solid colors — Red, Green, Blue, Yellow, White, Black, Pink, and Cyan — one at a time. Press any key to advance to the next color. Each color is paired with a distinct musical tone, generated in real time as a pure sine wave with a smooth fade-out, so there is no harsh click or jarring sound. Press ESC to exit.

That is the entire program. Your baby clicks, and the click produces a change right before their eyes: New color, new sound.

---

## How to Set It Up

The original setup, from 1994, is still the best one.

You are at your desk. You take a second monitor and a second keyboard — an old one you don't mind being grabbed and drooled on — and you put them on the floor or in a crib, whereever playspace is. You start the program. You let your baby crawl over.

The baby hits a key. The whole screen changes color. If you do it at night, the light in the whole *room* changes, if the monitor is big enough. A tone sounds. The baby stares. The baby hits another key.

That's all there's to it. That's the game.

## Who It Is For

**The sweet spot is 6 to 15 months.**

| Age | What to expect |
|---|---|
| Under 3 months | Too young — newborns have limited color perception and no motor control |
| 3–6 months | May respond to colors and sounds; but you, the parent, probably still has to teach how to do the key-pressing |
| **6–12 months** | **Ideal** — color recognition is developing, cause-and-effect is fascinating, repetition is delightful. Prepare for giggles. |
| **12–15 months** | **Still excellent** — baby may begin pressing keys independently and with intention |
| 18–24 months | Growing out of it — toddlers begin to want more complexity, animation, narrative |
| 24 months+ | Too simple on its own; consider adding features or graduating to more interactive programs |

## Why It Works

Human agency ("your actions can create change in the world around you") is one of the most important things to teach a new human. How do we, as parents, teach that to someone who can't yet walk and haven't even said their first word?

- **Immediate response** — no delay between keypress and color change. Cause and effect are unambiguous.
- **High contrast colors** — selected for visibility at a developmental stage when color perception is still maturing.
- **Audio paired with visual** — engages two senses simultaneously, supporting neural pathway development during a critical window.
- **Fullscreen** — the whole monitor changes. At floor level, on a CRT, that means the whole room changes. That is not a small thing to a baby.
- **No overstimulation** — no flashing, no animation, no narrative complexity. One thing changes at a time.

Even very young babies understand, at some level, that they caused this. That their hand on the keyboard made the room turn red, then black, then yellow, then green. That understanding — *I act, the world responds* — is foundational. It arrives before language and before walking, and this program makes it arrive with a keyboard on the floor or in a crib. 

---

## The Thing About a Favorite Color

Babies develop preferences early. Don't be surprised if yours finds a favorite color and starts waiting for it; hitting keys through the cycle until the right one appears, then pausing, sitting with it, before moving on.

Green is a common one. Something about the wavelength, perhaps, or the contrast. But it'll be whatever your baby decides it'll be, and you'll know it when you see them stop.

## On Screen Time and Infant Development

There is ongoing and legitimate debate about screen time for infants. The research landscape distinguishes between passive consumption and interactive, caregiver-mediated engagement. **Baby's First Computer** sits firmly in the latter category:

- A caregiver is present and engaged
- The content is responsive, not broadcast
- Sessions are naturally short, they stop because babies lose interest and move on
- There is no algorithm, no autoplay, and of course no commercial interest in prolonging engagement

The key difference from television or video: nothing happens unless someone in the room makes it happen. The baby learns that the screen is a surface that responds to them, rather than the usual passive feed of being a window onto a world that ignores them.

This program is the opposite of harmful to infant development. How much is too much? That's for you to decide. You're the parent, use your own judgment, as any good parent does.

## A Note from the Author

A good friend from the Cypherpunks group wrote the first version of this in 1994. Our first child had just been born, and he wanted her to learn agency as early as possible. He did that by making something for her that that said: *the computer knows you are here, you are in control, and it will respond to you.*

I set up a monitor and an old keyboard on the floor. My princess crawled over. Hit a key. The room lit up in a different color. We cycled through white, red, black, yellow, green — and when green came around, she waited, staring at it, and clearly loving that color. Then she hit the key again.

She's an adult now and she's a tech native who clearly in charge of how she massters computers. Technology is not in charge of her. 

The program, now remade, works just like it did more than three decades ago. If your baby grows up to be curious about how computers respond to people, and someday wants to read the source code, it is all there and simple enough to understand (or build upon) at a quick glance.

### Why Agency?

The earlier agency is taught, the better.  For the original author of this program, the goal was to transmit the lesson of human agency: The right of individuals to act, to cause effects, to not be passive recipients of what more powerful actors decide for them.

Before a baby has the words for it, they learn the lesson of having agency: A keyboard on the floor. Press a key. The room changes. *You did that.*

"Baby's First Computer" is simple and open source so any parent can give their child that lesson before the child has words for it. The original version was written by a member of the Cypherpunks, a group which wanted a world where individuals have power over their own lives. He gave a crawling baby a keyboard and a screen that responded to them, and the reason he wrote it was foundational: taking agency starts before language, before politics, before any of the battles that come later.

The child who learns at six months that their actions change the world is better equipped for entering their world on their own terms.  You don't have to be a cypherpunk to be the kind of parent who want your children to grow up to be free and independent beings with power over their own lives. Parents want to raise a competent human with the wherewithal to stand on their own feet and interact with the world (including with technology) as a doer, and someone who who was already, from the floor up, constituted as an individual who acts rather than waits, who reaches for the keyboard rather than watching the screen, and who knows instinctively, in their bones, that the world responds to them.

The cypherpunks set out to fix what they saw as problems, and they sought a revolution. But the cypherpunk who wrote the original version of this little game did it because he wanted his friend's baby to be raised as someone for whom problems wouldn't take hold in the first place. This meant raising a child who grew up with such a secure sense of their own agency that they would instinctively be skeptical of systems that want to make them passive. 

You don't have to care about politics or early internet ideology to put a keyboard on the floor and let a baby play with this program. You just have to believe that your child's first relationship with technology should be one where they are in charge of it, not the other way around.

That belief — unpolitical, instinctive, parental — is durable. The lesson a baby learns on the floor is where agency begins.
